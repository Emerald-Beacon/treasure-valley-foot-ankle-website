Heel Pain Treatment

Heel pain is one of the most common foot complaints we treat. Whether it's your first step in the morning or pain after a long day, we can identify the cause and get you relief.

Understanding Heel Pain

Heel pain affects millions of Americans and can significantly impact your quality of life. The heel bears most of your body weight with every step, making it vulnerable to injury and overuse.

Many people try to "walk it off" or hope it will go away on its own. While some heel pain does resolve, many cases require proper diagnosis and treatment. The good news is that most heel pain responds well to conservative treatment when addressed appropriately.

Common Causes of Heel Pain

Bottom of the Heel

  • Plantar Fasciitis: The most common cause - inflammation of the plantar fascia, causing stabbing pain with first morning steps
  • Heel Spurs: Bony growths on the underside of the heel bone, often associated with plantar fasciitis
  • Stone Bruise: Deep bruise of the fat pad covering the heel bone
  • Heel Fracture: Stress fractures or traumatic fractures of the heel bone
  • Baxter's Nerve Entrapment: Compression of a small nerve in the heel

Back of the Heel

  • Achilles Tendinopathy: Inflammation or degeneration of the Achilles tendon
  • Haglund's Deformity: Bony bump at the back of the heel (pump bump)
  • Bursitis: Inflammation of the bursa between the Achilles tendon and heel bone

Other Causes

  • Tarsal Tunnel Syndrome: Nerve compression causing heel and arch pain
  • Arthritis: Including rheumatoid arthritis and other inflammatory conditions
  • Peripheral Neuropathy: Nerve damage causing burning or tingling
  • Fat Pad Atrophy: Thinning of the protective cushion under the heel

When to See a Doctor

You should schedule an appointment if you have:

  • Heel pain that persists beyond a few weeks
  • Severe pain or swelling
  • Difficulty walking normally
  • Pain that occurs at rest or at night
  • Heel pain following an injury
  • Numbness, tingling, or fever with heel pain

Diagnosis

Proper diagnosis is essential for effective treatment. Dr. Johnson will:

  • Discuss your symptoms, medical history, and activities
  • Perform a physical examination of your foot and ankle
  • Observe your gait and foot biomechanics
  • Order X-rays to check for bone problems
  • Use ultrasound or MRI if soft tissue damage is suspected

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on the specific cause but often includes:

  • Rest and Activity Modification: Reducing stress on the heel
  • Ice: Reducing pain and inflammation
  • Stretching Exercises: Targeting specific muscles and tendons
  • Proper Footwear: Supportive shoes appropriate for your activities
  • Custom Orthotics: Addressing biomechanical issues
  • Night Splints: Maintaining stretch while you sleep
  • Physical Therapy: Guided rehabilitation
  • Medications: Anti-inflammatory medications
  • Injections: Corticosteroid or PRP therapy for targeted relief
  • Shockwave Therapy: Stimulating healing in chronic cases
  • Surgery: Rarely needed, but available when conservative treatment fails

Prevention Tips

  • Wear supportive shoes appropriate for your activities
  • Replace worn athletic shoes regularly
  • Stretch your feet and calves daily
  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Increase activity levels gradually
  • Listen to your body - don't ignore persistent pain

Tired of Heel Pain?

Don't let heel pain control your life. Most cases improve significantly with proper treatment.
